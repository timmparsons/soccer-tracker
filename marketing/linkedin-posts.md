## 1. World Cup final (topical — post Mon Jul 20, 2026)

Status: ready

The World Cup final was played in this country yesterday.

Millions of American kids watched it happen in their own time zone, maybe in their own city.

I keep thinking about what happens next.

In England, everyone my age remembers Italia '90. Not because of the result. Because of what we did the next morning — grabbed a ball and tried to be Gazza until it got dark.

The inspiration part is done. It was magnificent.

But inspiration has a shelf life of about a week.

The kids who'll still be different in October are the ones who had a ball at their feet on Monday.

## 2. Coaching observation

Status: ready

I asked my U12s at practice who'd touched a ball since Thursday.

Two hands went up. Out of eleven.

My first reaction was frustration. My second was that it's not their fault.

These kids have school, homework, piano, church, siblings' schedules. Their week is planned down to the half hour by adults.

Growing up in Bury, nobody planned anything for us. Which meant every evening was ours, and every evening had a ball in it.

American kids aren't less passionate. Their childhood just has no unscheduled space for the ball to live in.

You can't coach your way around that in two sessions a week.

## 3. The math one (educational)

Status: ready

I did some maths on my own training sessions.

An hour of team practice — with lines, water breaks, me talking too much — gets a kid maybe 300 touches on the ball.

Two sessions a week, thirty-week season. Call it 18,000 touches a year.

A kid who does ten minutes of footwork in the driveway every evening gets about 1,000 touches a day. That's 365,000 a year.

Twenty times more. From ten minutes a day nobody watches.

We spend so much energy debating training methods, curriculums, formations for nine-year-olds.

The biggest variable in player development might be a driveway.

## 4. Behind-the-scenes (founder story)

Status: ready — NOTE: decide whether to keep "Caleb" or swap for "his teammate" before posting

My son has touched a ball every day for 70 days straight.

I'd love to say it's because of my coaching. It isn't.

It's because there's a streak counter, and his teammate is above him on a leaderboard, and losing to Caleb on a Saturday is apparently unacceptable.

I built a little app for our team to track their touches. I thought the tracking was the product.

Turns out the product is one kid checking the leaderboard after dinner and going back outside.

Adults figured this out years ago. Nobody runs alone anymore — they run against their Strava feed.

Kids don't need more motivation than we did. They just need the game to exist between the games.

## 5. Origin story (post FIRST, then pin to profile)

Status: ready

I never planned to build an app.

I coach my son's U12 team in Arizona. I grew up in Bury, where football happened every night after school without a single adult involved.

For two years I tried to talk my players into practicing at home. Handouts. Challenges. Pep talks.

Nothing stuck, because a sheet of drills can't compete with a Nintendo Switch.

So I stopped trying to out-argue the video games and started borrowing from them. Streaks. Challenges. A leaderboard with their mates on it.

I'm not sure a coach from Bury building software in his kitchen is how player development gets fixed.

But eleven kids touched a ball yesterday who probably wouldn't have.

That'll do for now.
